More specifically, Telltale's original version of the game from 2005, still available on Steam to this day. The sheer simplicity and honesty of the gameplay and graphics is a sight to behold.

The visuals and setting

Clearly, the game is a sort of tech demo, probably made with the primary purpose of gaging their internal engine's capabilities. The graphics are a crude 3D that I am personally in love with, and the design of the characters really complements the primitive visuals. By 2005, the capabilties were generally much stronger for 3D, but seeing as this is a glorified tech demo that can now be purchased for the steep price of $1, they probably weren't too concerned with doing anything groundbreaking.

The music is pretty great, apparently written by a band called "Jerry's Jive", the website of which is linked in the credits (be warned, the website is not secure. A relic of the past!).

The characters

The character design is very caricature-esque, and their movements can be slightly uncanny, yet adorable. This is the kind of software design you don't really see these days; you can tell everything was designed and implemented by hand.

The four characters include two middle-aged men (Boris and Harry), a young surfer dude (the game provides his full name to be 'Theodore Dudebrough'), and a grandma, literally named "Grandma" in-game but apparently actually named "Grandma Shakey". That is, four characters not including yourself, The Player, who is described as "a mysterious player known only as 'The Player'" in the opening introduction to the game.

From what I can tell, Grandma is Theodore's grandma, albeit Thedore apparently hails from Las Vegas, Nevada while Grandma lives in Bowling Green, Kentucky. I've made this observation as Theodore will comment on Grandma's various husbands she's had throughout the years; it is possible Theodore simply knows Grandma as a friend, coworker, or otherwise, however.

Harry often brings up one "Artie Flopshark", who, in their universe, is apparently an absolute whiz at Texas Hold 'Em. Harry quotes him constantly. Not a real guy, though.

Grandma also appears to be a murder of some sort: Grandma: Why can't grandma ever get any love? Theodore: Maybe because you kill everyone who ever love- Harry: Shut up, Ted.

Occasionally, when you're taking too long to play a move, someone behind your view will yell out something akin to "What's taking so long?". This, alongisde the introduction of the players at the start, indicates that the game is being watched by an audience. I doubt this is truly professional, as the characters are pretty lousy at the game, at best.

Texas Hold 'Em

The game consists of exactly one mode. There's no switching to Blackjack or anything like that. There's one game, the same characters every time, in the same place every time, and that's Texas Hold 'Em.

If you've never played, the rule are simple, and this game would actually be a solid choid to learn them with. You're dealt two cards; everyone then chooses to bet, which they will do if their cards show some prowess, or they're simply bluffing. Two players are always given the burden of the big and small "blinds", which in this game are $200 and $100 respectively, which they place in the pot as a means of having some money in the pot which players are now competing for. The two players carrying this burden are alternated each game.

Players choose to fold, check, raise, or go all in. Folding takes you out of the game until the next one begins, and is typically done when a player believes the others have superior cards. Checking adds to the pot the total of the big blind. Raising adds an amount larger than the big blind. Going all in stakes all of your chips.

As far as I can tell, the characters' AI is not particularly intelligent. They're usually scared of the player going all-in, and tend to fold afterward. Not always, however. The characters also have personalities and tendencies; i.e., grandma fols right off the rip more often than not. Harry tends to stay in the longest, even to his detrimentt. This matches his incredibly cocky demeanor. Theodore is generally pretty honest and plays when he has good cards. Boris seems like a wildcard, but tends to fold. When a character wins, it's typically Harry or Theodore, at least in my experience thus far.

I would wager to guess that an experienced player can best these guys more often than not, albeit the entire idea of bluffing is sort of lost. I haven't really noticed the characters making particular statements or having any tells for when they're bluffing, either.

My thoughts

The game is alright for what it is. If you appreciate the old graphics and its overall character, then it's likely worth the sub-$1 you can purchase it for when it's on sale.

The beauty of semantic HTML

UTC 2025-04-08 - April 8th, 2025

Yea yea, the semantic web is a modern idea! What's old-school about that?

Well, I should first admit that I am a beginner at web development. I can make an okay static page... and that's about where my expertise ends. I want to get better of course, but I find myself really wanting to get the fundamentals down before I delve into anything beyond my feeble brain's capabilties.

And fortunately for me, I find HTML and CSS alone to be a lot of fun, and really interesting. I suppose it's the opposite for most, but I like "handmade" stuff, especially really good "handmade" stuff. I have yet to really delve into frameworks and the like, but it seems to me it might take my enjoyment out of things a bit. Again, just a me thing. The creation of a webpage using only essential tooling is oddly satisfying to me. Plus... it's old-school. :-)

Maybe that explains why I find HTML interesting. So many tags, yet we choose to just <div> things up and call it a day. As of writing this post, there are 123~ tags standardized in HTML5. This doesn't even include tags such as <marquee> (which Pipertext uses, if you haven't noticed) which are considered deprecated, albeit still often functional on most of the major browsers.

Why they're useful

First of all, inspect this page, open the <div> with a class of "container" and look at the stunning, beautiful, expertly-crafted layout. Hear that clapping coming from the other room? They just saw my layout too.

But seriously, it's just so clean, so easy to navigate. Sure, that's largely chalked up to my use of CSS grid, but it's still just so much prettier to my eye than a bunch of <div>s with class names designated differently among developers. See, there's my footer, it's in the <footer> tag. Woah dude, where's your sidebar?! Well, it's in the <aside> tag, of course!

Your CSS will be cleaner as a result as well. Just type main {} and you're good to go. I suppose it doesn't really matter if it's a codebase only you will be maintaining, but if everyone always wrote their HTML like this, maintaining other's work would probably become a million times easier.

Some of them are admittedly goofy and seem unnecessary. Take for instance the <dfn> tag, which is short for "definition element". It's supposed to represent a word that is to be defined within the context of some part of a page. I mean, cool... but is anyone actually going to remember to do that? Probably not. I would imagine tags like this do see their use in pages where accessibility is critical, however.

In conclusion, semantic HTML is simply <div>ine!

The Vive 100% energy drink

UTC 2025-12-20 - December 20th, 2025

I have a penchant for energy drinks. If you read the article on Grinds coffee pouches, you'll know that I made it clear that caffeine does nothing to me. Doesn't wake me up, doesn't relax me, nothing. Their unique flavor category is what draws me in; I love the taste commonly described as "battery acid". Personally, I've always seen energy drinks as a more refined form of soda. They typically have lighter carbonation and thus don't go flat like a typical soft drink.

Nearby me, there's a huge international market that sells all sorts of weird shit that American minds likely have a hard time comprehending, like edible sage. I didn't have a clue that was a thing until my most recent visit there. They also have a variety of energy drinks; some in a dedicated section with various soft drinks, some foreign from the US and some not, and some in the various nation-specific sections around the store. Vive 100% was found in the section dedicated to Mexico.

The bottle

Pictured above, the bottle resembles that of a sports drink more than a typical energy drink, in-line with Gatorade. Some energy drink companies in the US do have a history of trying to break into that market with products like Monster Hydro, as pictured adjacent to this paragraph. Currently, the only company that markets its products as "energy drinks" but also packages them in plastic bottles like that is Prime, one of the few energy drinks I've never actually had. Take a wild guess why I haven't raced to the nearest gas station to get myself a taste.

The motto for the product seems to be Energía para tu día a día, which translates to "Energy for your day-to-day", found just below the "Vive 100% Energy" logo. The bottle obviously appears most sports drink-esque, but the company is clearly trying to market this more broadly. The bottle's visuals aren't really too interesting otherwise.

The cap is truly strange. The picture shows the bottle already unwrapped, but the label goes around the green cap, which needs to be peeled off before taking off the lid. Interestingly, the green cap you see attached is not the actual cap; it is literally just a green cone that goes over the actual, more typical cap. As in, the green cone does not have threads on it, and can just be tossed after removal. Underneath of it is a standard water bottle cap that threads on and off. A gigantic waste of plastic.

The drink

As with most "original" energy drinks that don't list a specific flavor, it is very much in league with original Red Bull, Monster, and other originals. It's probably closest to Red Bull; it could be best described as a variation of it. Particularly, I'd say it's a bit less tart, and more sweet. A flavor that's hard to describe without tasting it for yourself, but if you've had Red Bull, and you liked it, then you'll like this.

The company directly states, "Thanks to the combination of guaraná and tea extract, you get a sustained burst of energy." The caffeine is thus likely natural, which some prefer.

On the web, I can see variations of this drink from Mexico and Colombia, but that's the extent of it. I'm unsure if it's sold in other Latin American countries, but I'm certain its sale doesn't extend past there. Other flavors like Borojó exist, but interestingly were not to be found at the market I went to. I imagine that original is the most popular flavor.

The nutrition facts

The nutrition facts are evidently a bit misleading on the sticker applied for the American market, pictured adjacent to this paragraph. I truly hate when they say there are six servings in a bottle like this; I doubt many people are drinking the bottle one sixth at a time throughout the week.

There's 30 grams total of sugar in it. It's low for a 16+ ounce energy drink, but you'll also notice that the ingredients list contains sucralose. It's a very sweet energy drink; it uses sucralose to make up for the relatively low amount of sugar in the bottle. For comparison, 16 ounces of Monster (the original) has 54 grams of sugar. This bottle is 20.28 ounces and contains only 30 grams, yet maintains a very sweet taste.

Slightly off-topic, but I really wish more energy drinks took this approach. 54 grams of sugar in one drink is a shit ton of sugar. On the other hand, I can immediately recognize sugar-free drinks when I have one, and it simply doesn't hit the same, and often yields a bitter aftertaste. Using less sugar and substituting another sweetener is quite literally the perfect middle ground. The only US market product I know that does this is Monster Lo-Carb.

The caffeine content is around 200 milligrams a bottle. Not obvious just off the nutrition facts; you have to do some math. They're using at least four different food colorings, which seems a bit much for a product in a mostly non-opaque bottle.

Final thoughts

A solid drink that I probably wouldn't buy again, at least not in the near future. It's not good enough to warrant repeat purchases, but it's definitely something I could see someone preferring over Red Bull.

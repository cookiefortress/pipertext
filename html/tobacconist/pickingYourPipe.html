Picking your pipe

this article is a part of the tobacconist series

Howdy piper! In this article, I'm hoping to give a short debrief on picking your pipe. It can seem like a lot to pick out your first one looking at the sheer amount of options there are. And if you're anything like me, you'll have some Einstein-level questions such as:

  • How does a pipe made of wood not catch on fire?
  • Who the hell first thought to make a pipe out of a corncob?
  • How do I look out for ACME exploding pipes? Elmer warned me about those...

Actually, I won't really be answering any of those. And anyways, what do you mean my briar pipes aren't supposed to double as a fireplace when I smoke with them? Nonetheless, I can certainly tell you how to get started with a fine smoking instrument!

What kinds of pipes are there?

At first, it may seem like there's a million different materials out of which pipes are made. But honestly, there's not very many:

  • Briar wood
  • Hardwoods (cherry, pear, etc.)
  • Morta (wood)
  • Other woods (larch, ash)
  • Corncob
  • Meerschaum
  • Clay
  • Calabash
  • Metal
  • Miscellaneous novelties

Any materials besides those listed above/below should warrant some caution when being used for making tobacco pipes. The most common material is, by far, briar. Despite my introduction, I will answer one of those questions: briar wood doesn't burn until it reaches around 1000 degrees Celsius. Also, hardwood used for pipes generally catches fire at around 600-700 degrees Celsisus. Smoking pipes usually reach around 500 degrees celsisus, depending on the cadence of the smoker.

But which one is right for you? I won't provide too many external links in this article, as I just want to provide my general opinion on what will get the job done for you. I'll give some general price estimates and what pipe works best for what.

Briar pipes

Briar pipes are basically the gold standard, as much as it pains to me say that as a corncob elitist. They just work; they generally burn pretty cool, depending on the specific pipe. They're also quite varied in appearance; in terms of pipe shapes, there isn't one out there which isn't made with briar.

There's not really a downside to them, honestly. They're really quite durable compared to other materials. A briar pipe taking a fall may scuff it, but it's not going to render it unusable. It's also going to be hard to smoke so hot that you do any damage to them; try not to get that baby too hot, of course, but that applies to any pipe. Briars really are the jack-of-all-trades of the pipe world, and they may even be the master of some!

The price of one will vary wildly. At a certain point, you're paying for craftsmanship, brand recognition, or other novelties. A Dr. Grabow will realistically smoke just as good as a Peterson, if not better, depending on your preferences. For a brand new, low-end briar, you can expect to pay $40-$50. Not too punishing, but certainly not the cheapest option, as we'll see later! Keep in mind that estate pipes (exquisite term for used pipes) are always cheaper than new ones, so long as you don't mind the idea of them. I've bought them and had good experiences, personally! No break-in required!

Hardwood pipes

The red-headed stepchild of pipe materials, hardwood is very underrated, in my opinion. It's most commonly found in lower-end Missouri Meerschaum pipes, but high-end hardwood pipes (which can be of varieties like strawberry or olive woods) are also plentiful. In my experience, these are amazing smokers, albeit I've only ever smoked ones sub-$30, so my experience isn't particularly broad, and I can't speak to the pricier ones.

Hardwood pipes tend to be lighter compared to their briar friends, but this can vary wildly, being largely dependent on the density of the specific hardwood. In general, it would be safe to say that hardwood pipes offer an easier clenching experience. Yet, they are also more prone to scuffs from drops or knocking into things, especially given their lighter color will make those scuffs really shine.

Hardwood pipes, as I mentioned above, vary in terms of price. I recommend picking up a Missouri Meerschaum one, they make various kinds like this one (which I personally own) which are really great smokers. If you want a cheaper pipe but can't swallow the hillbilly aesthetics of corncob pipes, then these are the way to go. High-end hardwood pipes can run you into the $100+ price range, though!

Morta pipes

I will be honest: I had never heard of morta before working on this article page. Now that I know about it, though, my wallet feels a bit heavy... I think it could use some help with that...

Morta wood (which is known as bog-wood more generally) is a wood that has been buried in decayed vegetation for hundreds if not thousands of years. According to the Wikipedia article I linked, morta is basically wood in the early stages of fossilisation; leave it there for a few million extra years and it'll turn into coal.

I'm no researcher, but from what I can tell, this wood offers some impressive durability, generally preferable even to briar. It is extremely heat-resistant, and also very beautiful; the patterns are absolutely gorgeous, and I love the natural grey color it often presents.

Surprisingly, these aren't the priciest things in the world; here's one that'll run you around the $150 dollar range. Probably not the best option if you're just starting out, but they're cool to know about, not only for their use in pipes but also in general. And the name is badass. Bog-wood.

Other woods

There's various other woods that are often used in pipes. I won't go too into detail here, but here's an ash wood pipe and here's a larch wood pipe. These are pretty pricey, and similar to morta pipes, probably aren't the best option if you're just starting out. If you become invested in the hobby, I'm sure these make for cool pieces in the ol' collection, and probably great smokers too.

Corncob pipes

The holy grail of pipes, corncobs can do no wrong. Cool smoking and cool looking, affordable yet durable, and utterly stylish. There's simply nothing more classy than a corncob pipe.

Maybe I'm exaggerating a bit. Most people wouldn't call it the "holy grail". I just figured I'd let you know of my biases ahead of time. Even though I'm right. But seriously, the above points are mostly correct, apart from the last one which is up for debate. Personally, I've never thought corncob pipes look bad. I think they look cool, and they make ones that don't resemble a corncob anyways.

The big plus for these is that they're not only cheap, but pretty damn durable, and hard to break. I have this cob, and it's my primary smoker. I've dropped it, banged it into crap, and I smoke pretty hot so it's taken a beating on that front too. It's my best smoker; better than my Peterson, and better than my Rossi, to me. I can't vouch for cobs enough.

Nonetheless, I can understand that some perhaps can't get past the aesthetics of it. Even with stained cobs with are brown, black, or another less corncob-esque color, I would be above my head to say that I might feel a bit ridiculous smoking out of certain pipes myself. However, if that's no issue for you, then cobs all the way for your first smokin' pipe!

I recommend checking out the Missouri Meerschaum website and just taking a look around at what they have. The prices listed are sometimes beat by vendors, but their website has the most exhaustive selection of pipes. Their cobs range from sub-$10 to $50+, but you're mostly paying for visuals and larger bowl sizes at higher price ranges. If you're lucky, you'll catch them when they're selling packs of seven blemished pipes for around $50!

A note of warning; I wouldn't buy corncob pipes from basically anyone besides Missouri Meerschaum. They're likely made with dubious materials in foreign nations for very cheap, and with very cheap labor. Missouri Meerschaum pipes are cheap enough to warrant never buying something any cheaper. You may wonder why I'm implying they're the only corncob pipe company around, and that's because they basically are. There's also Old Dominion, but that's a bit of a moot point as they are owned by Missouri Meerschaum! Truthfully, all of the other cob companies were ran out of business sometime in the last century or two. There's some small vendors that make handmade corncob pipes (usually found on Etsy and the like), but other than them, stay skeptical!

Meerschaum

As of writing this article, I don't own a meerschaum pipe. I've never even tried one; but I can still tell you what I know!

Their primary advantage is that they are very porous, which means a lot of the moisture and tarcontent are drawn into stone rather than into your mouth. Not necessarily a "safer" smoke, but many argue that it enhances the flavor. It also doesn't burn, period; they offer a very heat-resistant smoke.

The thing with a lot of the meerschaum pipe market is that you either love them or hate them; check this out for an example. Don't get me wrong, anyone can tell you that handmade beauties like this are works of art and a really cool display of craftsmanship, but I will tell you personally that I wouldn't want to smoke out of it! Not only for fear of damaging it, but also because I do find it slightly corny. But who the hell am I? If you dig 'em, buy 'em! They make plenty of them which aren't shaped like faces, ships, or whatever, as well.

Their weight can vary, depending on how hard the meerschaum is pressed, and the variety of meerschaum. In general, though, they aren't as heavy as they may look in pictures.

These pipes do tend to run on the pricier side, so these aren't the best option for a new piper.

Clay

Perhaps the most impractical of the options thus far. If you think a corncob pipe makes you look old-timey, well, look again.

For reference, this is a pretty typical clay pipe. They come in other colors of course, like white or brown. Some have clay bowls while still having metal and plastics on the shank and stem.

Clay pipes are unique, and they're not for everyone. For starters, don't drop them. Or hit them off anything. Or look at them. Because they're fragile, and they will break! They also smoke very hot; even the slowest piper in the world will burn the shit out of their hand at the touch of one of these bad boys.

The benefit is that they require no break-in period, and are completely neutral in terms of flavor; that is, unlike cobs and briars, you probably won't sense the pipe itself imparting any sort of its natural flavor into your tobacco. You also look cool. But you look cool with every pipe, let's be honest.

I have the exact clay pipe linked above. They really are not punishing price-wise, but as with most of the other materials I've already talked about, they can get pricey for the higher-end stuff. They're cheap enough to warrant a purchase of one, even if it's just to look cool on the ol' pipe cabinet. Probably not the best beginner pipe, but if you're into the aesthetic I say go for it. Just do beware: don't burn your fingers into tiny puddles of human ash.

Calabash

Calabash pipes are some of the most interesting out there, in my opinion. Do note: calabash can refer non-calabash pipes that are simply shaped like actual calabash pipes, like this.

Allow me to level with you here, and say that I cannot find a single one from a reputable retailer that I'd feel safe linking to here. They're novel little pieces, especially if you can find one, apparently. They were more commonplace in olden times, when the pipe market was much bigger. It seems there's plenty that are made but still use a briar or meerschaum chamber, but in terms of pure calabash, I didn't have much luck. It's easy to find pictures of others' antique ones on various forums though.

Metal

I will come out swinging here; metal makes for a dogshit pipe. I have smoked out of approximately one, and while it was the price of a low-end corncob pipe, my expectations were pulled down from the ceiling and slammed through the ground so hard that they gave a quick greeting to Satan on the way down. No flavor, burns hot as shit, and generally just feels cheap. You'll probabyl only find these in little tobacco shops for dirt cheap. I imagine these are more for the Devil's lettuce rather than any tobacco, and even then, use something classier!

Just to clarify, I am not talking about Falcon pipes, which are pipe stems that you can buy interchangeable bowls to use with. Falcon makes neat pipes that are beloved by pipers all over. The problem is specifically with metal bowls/chambers, where the tobacco is being burnt.

Novelty materials

At the end of the day, a pipe can be made out of anything. That doesn't mean that a pipe should be made out of anything, but it's a big world, with billions of us in it; people have just about tried everything! You can check out this page on Pipedia which goes a bit more in-depth regarding the different materials used in pipes, but I'll also briefly go over some of them below.

On the Pipedia page linked just above, it provides a mostly exhaustive list of the woods you can easily find pipes made with. Note that it appears to be missing some, like ash wood. For some reason, I am an absolute sucker for pipes made of unique woods. It makes my briars just seem so boring when I could be smoking out of some leadwood! (wait, lead..???)

Pipes have been made out of porcelain, although it seems they are more of an antique now. I can only imagine how hot these suckers get, and I can also only imagine the amount of shit that would run down my leg if I dropped it on my foot. A quick google search showed some people selling handmade ones, if that piques your interest at all.

Stone is another one. Yep, stone. They still make them, too, although it looks like the chambers are quite narrow. I believe these are intended to be historically accurate, and Native Americans did not smoke their tobacco like people do now; it was (as far as I'm aware) entirely for ceremonial purposes in most cases. Thus, they didn't need a large chamber.

Well, there's certainly others, but given this was just supposed to be an introduction to pipes for the novice piper, I may have overextended my reach a bit. I hope you were able to get some value out of this! As always, any errors or suggestions you have, send them my way on the contact page!
